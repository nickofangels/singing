## 1. VIBRATO

### 1.1 Definition and Acoustic Characteristics

Vibrato in singing is a quasi-periodic modulation of fundamental frequency (f0), typically accompanied by correlated modulations in amplitude and timbre. Sundberg (1994, "Perceptual Aspects of Singing," Music Perception) characterized vibrato by two primary parameters:

- **Rate**: the frequency of the modulation cycle, measured in Hz
- **Extent**: the depth of pitch excursion, measured in cents or semitones (peak-to-peak)

#### Normative Values

Sundberg's extensive acoustic analyses established widely cited norms:

- **Rate**: approximately 5.5-6.5 Hz in Western classical singing. The most commonly cited central value is ~6 Hz. Sundberg (1987, "The Science of the Singing Voice," Northern Illinois University Press) reported a mean of approximately 6.0 Hz across trained classical singers.
- **Extent**: approximately +/- 1 semitone (roughly +/- 50-70 cents on each side of the mean pitch, yielding a total excursion of ~100-140 cents peak-to-peak). Sundberg reported typical extents of approximately +/- 0.5 to 1 semitone.

Prame (1994, JASA, "Measurements of the Vibrato Rate of Ten Singers") analyzed vibrato characteristics in recordings of professional opera singers. Found mean rates clustered around 5.5-6.5 Hz across singers, with individual consistency — each singer had a characteristic rate that remained relatively stable across different pitches, dynamics, and vowels. Extent showed more variability than rate.

Dejonckere et al. (1995, Folia Phoniatrica et Logopaedica) found that vibrato rate is relatively stable within an individual singer across different phonatory conditions, supporting the idea that rate is a relatively fixed neuromuscular property.

#### Perceptual Aspects

Sundberg (1994) demonstrated that listeners perceive the mean pitch of a vibrato tone, not the moment-to-moment pitch — the auditory system integrates across the modulation cycle. This perceptual averaging is thought to operate optimally within the 5-7 Hz range. Below ~4 Hz, the ear begins to track individual pitch excursions (perceived as a "wobble"). Above ~8 Hz, the modulation blurs into a tense, tight quality ("tremolo" or "bleat").

Shipp et al. (1980, Journal of Voice) found that listeners rated vibrato with rates of 5-7 Hz and moderate extent as "pleasing" or "beautiful," while deviations in either direction were rated negatively.

### 1.2 Physiological Mechanism

The mechanism of vibrato has been debated for over a century. Three primary origin theories have been investigated:

#### 1.2.1 Laryngeal Origin Theory

The dominant modern view is that singing vibrato originates primarily from modulation of cricothyroid (CT) muscle activity, producing periodic changes in vocal fold length/tension and thus f0.

**Key evidence:**

- Hirano et al. (1995, Journal of Voice) and Titze et al. used EMG (electromyography) to record CT activity during vibrato. CT showed periodic bursts at the vibrato rate (~6 Hz), with the thyroarytenoid (TA) showing either in-phase or anti-phase periodic activity.
- Titze, Story, and Laukkanen (various, summarized in Titze, "Principles of Voice Production," 1994/2000, NCVS) proposed that vibrato arises from an oscillatory loop in the CT-TA antagonist muscle pair. The CT stretches the fold (raising pitch); the TA contracts slightly to resist this stretch; the resulting oscillation in tension produces f0 modulation.
- Ludlow et al. (1986, Annals of Otology, Rhinology, and Laryngology) found that botulinum toxin injection into the CT muscle eliminated vibrato, strongly supporting CT involvement. Similarly, injection into the TA altered vibrato characteristics.

**Muscle tremor model (Titze):** Titze proposed that vibrato exploits the natural physiological tremor frequency of the laryngeal muscles. The ~6 Hz vibrato rate corresponds closely to the physiological tremor frequency of striated muscle (5-8 Hz range). In this view, trained singers learn to allow and shape a natural neuromuscular oscillation that untrained or tense singers suppress through co-contraction.

#### 1.2.2 Neural/Central Origin Theory

Vennard (1967, "Singing: The Mechanism and the Technic") and earlier researchers proposed a central nervous system oscillator driving vibrato. Under this view, a neural pacemaker in the brainstem or motor cortex generates the periodic signal.

Evidence for this view:
- The consistency of vibrato rate within individuals and across different phonatory tasks suggests a central timing mechanism.
- Seashore (1938, "Psychology of Music") documented vibrato rate consistency across years within individual singers.

However, the pure central oscillator theory has fallen out of favor because:
- EMG evidence shows the modulation occurs in the laryngeal muscles themselves
- The rate matches peripheral muscle tremor frequencies
- Central origin alone does not explain why vibrato develops with training rather than being present from the start

#### 1.2.3 Respiratory Origin Theory

Some researchers proposed that subglottal pressure oscillations drive vibrato. Respiratory muscles could generate periodic pressure fluctuations that modulate f0.

Evidence:
- Sundberg (1987) measured subglottal pressure variations during vibrato and found them to be small and likely a consequence rather than a cause of the laryngeal modulation. Pressure fluctuations during vibrato are typically 1-2 cmH2O, much smaller than would be required to produce the observed f0 excursions directly.
- Titze and Story (2002, JASA) modeled the interaction and concluded that while respiratory oscillations may exist, they are secondary to the laryngeal mechanism. The respiratory contribution is "along for the ride."

#### 1.2.4 Current Consensus

The prevailing view in voice science (Titze, 2000; Sundberg, 1987; Horii, 1989) is that vibrato is primarily a **laryngeal phenomenon** involving oscillation in the CT-TA antagonist pair, at a rate matching the natural physiological tremor frequency of striated muscle. The nervous system provides the excitatory drive, and respiration may contribute secondary modulations, but the oscillation is generated at the laryngeal level.

### 1.3 Vibrato Amplitude and Timbre Modulation

The f0 modulation of vibrato produces correlated modulations in:

- **Amplitude**: As f0 sweeps up and down, harmonics move relative to formant peaks, causing periodic amplitude fluctuations. The amplitude modulation depends on the harmonic-formant relationship at any given pitch and vowel.
- **Timbre/spectral envelope**: Same mechanism — the shifting harmonics produce spectral changes at the vibrato rate. This spectral modulation may be an important part of the "richness" perception of vibrato (Sundberg, 1995).

Horii (1989, Journal of Speech and Hearing Research) developed methods for independently measuring f0, amplitude, and spectral modulation in vibrato. Found that all three parameters modulate at the same rate but may differ in phase relationships.

### 1.4 Vibrato Development and Training

#### Developmental Trajectory

Vibrato is typically absent or irregular in beginning singers and emerges with training:

- Howard and colleagues (various publications, Journal of Voice) tracked vocal development in choir settings and found that vibrato regularity increases with years of training.
- Dromey et al. (2003, Journal of Voice) measured vibrato characteristics in singers with different training levels. Found that trained singers produced more regular vibrato (lower jitter in rate and extent) compared to untrained voices.
- Child voices typically lack vibrato. It emerges variably during adolescence and vocal maturation, though training accelerates and regularizes it.

#### Is Vibrato Trainable?

This is a nuanced question in the literature:

- **Vibrato regularity** is clearly trainable. Trained singers show more periodic, consistent vibrato than untrained singers.
- **Vibrato presence** appears to emerge as a byproduct of efficient, free laryngeal function rather than through direct training of the modulation itself. Multiple pedagogy traditions describe vibrato as "appearing when tension is released" rather than being directly produced.
- **Rate** appears relatively fixed within an individual and may be a constitutional trait (Prame, 1994). Training does not substantially change a singer's characteristic rate.
- **Extent** is more modifiable. Singers can learn to increase or decrease vibrato extent to some degree, and some degree of voluntary control over extent is achievable.

Titze (NCVS workshops, various) has described vibrato as emerging when the laryngeal muscles are operating in a "sweet spot" of activation — enough CT drive to create tension, with TA providing a yielding resistance, and without the co-contraction that locks out oscillation. Excess tension from either muscle suppresses the natural tremor-frequency oscillation.

King and Horii (1993, Journal of Voice) examined the ability of singers to modify vibrato parameters voluntarily. Singers could suppress vibrato (produce straight tone) and could modify extent to some degree, but had limited ability to alter rate.

### 1.5 Straight Tone

Straight tone (vibrato-less singing) is used in:
- Early music / Renaissance performance practice
- Many choral traditions (for blend and intonation precision)
- Some contemporary commercial music (CCM) styles

**Mechanism:** Straight tone requires either (a) increased co-contraction of CT and TA, "locking out" the oscillatory interaction, or (b) reduced overall laryngeal muscle activation below the threshold where oscillation occurs. Sundberg and colleagues noted that straight tone typically involves increased muscular effort (hypothesis a), which may explain why some voice teachers consider extended straight-tone singing potentially fatiguing.

Titze (2000) suggested that the increased effort required to maintain straight tone is a result of suppressing a natural oscillatory tendency of the laryngeal system. The relaxed, efficient larynx tends to oscillate; straight tone requires active suppression.

**Acoustic implications:** Vibrato contributes to the perception of warmth and richness. Sundberg demonstrated that vibrato causes the voice to excite more of the vocal tract resonance bandwidth, producing a "richer" spectral impression. Straight tone can sound "thinner" but allows more precise intonation perception.

### 1.6 Pathological Vibrato Variants

#### Wobble

- Rate slower than ~4 Hz, with normal or excessive extent
- Associated with aging voice (presbyphonia), loss of muscle tone in CT/TA
- Sataloff (1991, "Professional Voice: The Science and Art of Clinical Care") describes vocal wobble as correlated with atrophy or denervation of intrinsic laryngeal muscles
- Perceptually identified as "unsteady" or "wavering"

#### Tremolo / Bleat

- Rate faster than ~7-8 Hz, often with reduced extent
- Associated with excessive laryngeal tension, hyperfunction
- Can occur in anxious or fatigued singers
- Sundberg noted that the perceptual threshold between "vibrato" and "tremolo" is approximately 7.5 Hz rate

#### Clinical Vocal Tremor

Essential vocal tremor is a neurological condition distinct from singing vibrato. It involves involuntary modulation of f0 at ~4-7 Hz that the speaker cannot suppress, driven by central rather than peripheral mechanisms. Barkmeier-Kraemer and colleagues (Journal of Voice, various) distinguish essential tremor from singer's vibrato by the inability to suppress it and its presence in speech as well as singing.

### 1.7 Vibrato in Non-Western Traditions

Vibrato parameters differ across musical traditions:
- Japanese traditional singing (min'yo, nagauta) uses faster, narrower vibrato than Western classical singing (Tsuji et al., various)
- Indian classical singing employs ornamental pitch oscillations (gamaka, andolan) that differ mechanistically from Western vibrato
- Korean pansori uses a characteristic vibrato with different rate-extent combinations than Western norms

These cross-cultural differences suggest that while the physiological substrate is universal, the specific expression of vibrato is shaped by musical training and aesthetic preferences.

## 2. BELT TECHNIQUE

### 2.1 Definition and Context

Belt (or belting) is a vocal technique predominantly used in musical theater and contemporary commercial music (CCM) characterized by a powerful, speech-like, "calling" quality in the upper pitch range. It is acoustically and physiologically distinct from both classical head voice and untrained yelling.

LoVetri, Sundberg, and others have worked to bring scientific rigor to a technique that was historically controversial in voice pedagogy circles, with some classical teachers viewing it as inherently damaging.

### 2.2 Acoustic Characteristics

#### Sundberg, Titze, and Colleagues

Sundberg and Thalén (2010, Journal of Voice, "What is 'Twang'?") and related papers established key acoustic features of belt:

- **Dominant lower harmonics**: In belt, the first and especially the second harmonic (2f0) carry more energy relative to higher harmonics compared to classical singing at the same pitch.
- **Spectral slope**: Belt has a shallower spectral slope (less roll-off of higher harmonics) compared to classical head voice, contributing to the brighter, more "forward" quality.
- **Formant tuning**: The first formant (R1/F1) is tuned to reinforce the second harmonic (2f0) — written as R1:2f0 tuning. This differs from the classical soprano strategy of R1:f0 tuning at the highest pitches.

Titze and Worley (2009, Journal of the Acoustical Society of America) analyzed belt and opera singing acoustically and found that belt maintains a higher closed quotient (CQ) and higher H1-H2 differences than classical production at comparable pitches, consistent with a more pressed or "thyroarytenoid-dominant" phonation pattern.

#### The "Megaphone" Vocal Tract Shape

Titze (2001, Journal of the Acoustical Society of America) described belt as employing a "megaphone" vocal tract configuration: wide mouth opening, raised larynx, and relatively narrowed pharynx. This configuration:
- Raises the first formant frequency (allowing R1:2f0 tuning at higher pitches)
- Produces a brighter, more speech-like timbre
- Contrasts with the classical "inverted megaphone" (narrow mouth, lowered larynx, widened pharynx)

### 2.3 Laryngeal Mechanism in Belt

#### M1 Carried High

The defining laryngeal characteristic of belt is the use of mechanism 1 (M1, chest register / thyroarytenoid-dominant vibration) at pitches that would typically transition to M2 (head/falsetto) in classical or untrained singing.

- Schutte and Miller (1993, Journal of Voice) were among the first to document electromyographically and acoustically that belt involves maintaining the M1 (chest) laryngeal configuration above the typical M1-M2 transition pitch.
- In female belters, this means maintaining M1 up to approximately C5-E5 (523-659 Hz) or even higher, pitches at which classical female singers would be firmly in M2.
- The TA muscle remains more active than in classical head voice production at equivalent pitches, maintaining shorter, thicker vocal folds.

#### CT/TA Balance

In belt:
- **TA** remains highly active, maintaining vocal fold thickness and a more rectangular glottal waveform with higher closed quotient
- **CT** also activates strongly to achieve the high pitches, creating high vocal fold tension
- The result is a high-tension, high-contact configuration distinct from classical head voice (where CT dominates and folds are thinner/more stretched)

Kochis-Jennings et al. (2012, Journal of Voice) used laryngeal EMG to compare muscle activity in belt, classical (head voice), and speech-like chest production. Found that belt showed the highest TA:CT ratio of the three conditions at matched pitches above the typical passaggio, confirming the "chest register carried high" characterization.

### 2.4 Subglottic Pressure and Airflow

Belt typically involves:
- Higher subglottic pressure than classical singing at the same pitch and loudness (Sundberg et al., Journal of Voice)
- Lower mean airflow due to higher closed quotient (more complete glottal closure per cycle)
- Higher collision forces between vocal folds due to the combination of higher pressure and fuller adduction

Titze (2006, NCVS) noted that the higher collision forces in belt are partially offset by the shorter vibratory cycle at the high pitches where belt operates — higher f0 means shorter time in contact per cycle, which may reduce cumulative tissue loading compared to what would be predicted from subglottic pressure alone.

### 2.5 LoVetri's Contributions

Jeannette LoVetri has been a central figure in bringing belt into legitimate pedagogical and scientific discourse:

- LoVetri and Weekly (2003, Journal of Voice, "Contemporary Commercial Music (CCM) Survey: Who's Teaching What in Nonclassical Music") documented that classical voice training was the near-universal approach in academic institutions, despite most professional singing employment being in CCM styles. This highlighted a training gap.
- LoVetri (2008, Journal of Singing) articulated functional training principles for belt that distinguish it from both classical technique and untrained vocal production. Key principles include:
  - Belt must be trained as its own coordination, not as "modified classical" or "loud chest"
  - Laryngeal position, formant tuning, and registration strategy differ from classical norms and require specific training
  - Attempting to belt using classical technique settings (lowered larynx, covered vowels) produces a hybrid that is neither efficient belt nor classical production

- LoVetri coined/popularized the term "Contemporary Commercial Music" (CCM) to replace "non-classical" as the umbrella term, arguing that defining genres by what they are not is scientifically and pedagogically unhelpful.

### 2.6 Belt vs. Yelling: A Critical Distinction

Voice science distinguishes belt from yelling on multiple parameters:

| Parameter | Belt | Yelling |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Subglottic pressure | High but regulated | Unregulated, often excessive |
| Formant tuning | Strategic R1:2f0 alignment | No systematic tuning |
| Vocal tract shape | Consistent megaphone configuration | Variable, often constricted |
| Breath support | Active respiratory antagonism | Often absent; clavicular breathing |
| Closed quotient | High but stable | Erratically high |
| Supraglottic tension | Varies; ideally low | Typically high (false fold compression) |
| Dynamic range | Controlled crescendo/decrescendo possible | Near-maximum effort at all times |
| Sustainability | Trained belters perform 8 shows/week | Sustained yelling produces rapid fatigue/injury |

Titze and LoVetri have both emphasized that the biomechanical differences between belt and yelling are analogous to the difference between athletic technique and untrained exertion — the forces involved may be similar, but the distribution and control differ fundamentally.

### 2.7 Is Belt Inherently Damaging?

This has been one of the most debated questions in voice pedagogy. The scientific evidence suggests:

**Arguments that belt carries elevated risk:**
- Higher collision forces per cycle compared to classical production at the same pitch (Jiang and Titze, 1994, JASA)
- Higher subglottic pressure implies greater mechanical loading
- Prolonged M1 use in the upper range increases TA activity and may increase fatigue
- Epidemiological data (Phyland et al., 1999, Journal of Voice) showed higher rates of vocal pathology in musical theater performers compared to opera singers, though confounding variables (amplification practices, performance frequency, speaking demands) complicate interpretation

**Arguments that belt can be done safely:**
- Professional belters (Broadway performers) sustain 8-show weeks for years without developing pathology, suggesting safe production is possible
- Titze's modeling (2006) suggests that high f0 in belt partially compensates for high collision forces by reducing contact time per cycle
- Formant tuning in efficient belt reduces the subglottic pressure needed for a given loudness, compared to un-tuned (yelling-like) production
- Studies of trained belters show lower rates of supraglottic constriction (false vocal fold compression) compared to untrained belters (Yanagisawa et al., 1991, via laryngoscopy)

**Current consensus (approximate):**
Belt is a high-demand vocal technique that can be performed safely with proper training, adequate vocal health maintenance (hydration, rest, vocal hygiene), and appropriate amplification. It is not inherently more damaging than heavy classical singing (dramatic soprano, Wagnerian repertoire) when performed with good technique. The risk of injury increases with: inadequate training, dehydration, vocal fatigue, reflux, insufficient amplification requiring excess effort, and attempting to belt with classical or untrained technique settings.

Titze (2008, NCVS) summarized: efficient belt is characterized by high vocal fold impact stress but also by acoustic power transfer efficiency via formant tuning. The ratio of output power to collision dose is what determines safety, not collision dose alone.

### 2.8 Training Considerations for Belt

Scientific literature on belt training emphasizes:

- Belt must be trained specifically and progressively, not "discovered" through pushing chest voice higher (LoVetri, 2008)
- Speech-quality vowels and raised larynx are part of the technique, not faults to be corrected (Titze and Worley, 2009)
- Formant tuning awareness (even if implicit) is essential — the singer must learn vocal tract shapes that produce R1:2f0 alignment
- Semi-occluded vocal tract exercises serve as useful warm-up and recovery tools for belters, reducing the collision dose of the warm-up period (Titze, 2006)
- Belt training benefits from working on both M1 and M2 independently before combining, to ensure the singer has register flexibility and does not become locked into M1 for all upper-range production (LoVetri, functional voice training framework)

## 3. VOWEL MODIFICATION (VOWEL TUNING / COVERING)

### 3.1 The Problem: F0 vs. F1

In speech, vowel identity is determined primarily by the first two formant frequencies (F1 and F2). These formant frequencies are determined by vocal tract shape (tongue position, jaw opening, lip rounding) and are largely independent of fundamental frequency.

In singing, a problem arises as pitch rises: when f0 exceeds the natural first formant frequency (F1) of a vowel, that formant can no longer reinforce the fundamental or its lowest harmonics. The result is a loss of resonance support, reduced vocal power, and distorted vowel quality.

This is the central problem that vowel modification addresses.

### 3.2 The Soprano Problem

The issue is most acute for sopranos singing above the staff (roughly above C5/523 Hz):

- The vowel /i/ ("ee") has a typical speech F1 of ~270 Hz (female)
- The vowel /u/ ("oo") has a typical speech F1 of ~300 Hz
- By C5 (523 Hz), f0 already exceeds F1 for these closed vowels
- By C6 (1046 Hz), f0 exceeds F1 for almost all vowels

Sundberg (1977, JASA, "The Acoustics of the Singing Voice") demonstrated that sopranos systematically raise F1 by opening the jaw and modifying tongue position as pitch ascends, keeping F1 at or above f0. This is the R1:f0 tuning strategy.

**Consequence:** At very high pitches, all vowels converge toward an open /a/-like quality because the jaw must open maximally to raise F1 high enough. This is why soprano text becomes less intelligible above the staff — the vowel space collapses. Sundberg documented this convergence acoustically in several studies.

Joliveau, Smith, and Wolfe (2004, JASA, "Vocal Tract Resonances in Singing: The Soprano Voice") used real-time resonance measurement to confirm that trained sopranos tune R1 to track f0 across their range, and that the tuning is precise — typically within about 10% of f0. They also found that untrained sopranos show less consistent tuning.

### 3.3 Formant Tuning in Other Voice Types

#### Tenors

Tenors face a similar but less extreme version of the soprano problem. The passaggio region (approximately E4-G4, 330-392 Hz) is where f0 approaches and exceeds F1 for closed vowels. Tenor vowel modification typically involves:

- Opening closed vowels (e.g., /i/ toward /I/ or toward schwa-like positions)
- Modifying /e/ toward /ɛ/
- Raising F1 through jaw opening while maintaining vocal tract length via slight laryngeal lowering (classical approach) or maintaining speech posture (CCM approach)

Miller (1986, "The Structure of Singing," Schirmer/Macmillan) produced detailed vowel modification charts showing systematic modification paths for each vowel as pitch ascends through the passaggio and above. Miller's charts are empirically derived from analysis of successful tenor singing and show:

- /i/ modifies through /I/ toward /y/-colored schwa
- /e/ modifies through /ɛ/ toward /œ/ or open /ɛ/
- /a/ remains relatively stable longest but may brighten slightly
- /o/ modifies through /ɔ/
- /u/ modifies through /ʊ/ toward /o/-like positions

Miller noted these modifications are gradual, beginning below the passaggio and intensifying through and above it. Abrupt vowel changes produce audible register breaks.

#### Baritones and Basses

Lower voices face the F1-f0 conflict at lower pitches in their upper range. The same principles apply: F1 must be raised as f0 approaches it, requiring progressive jaw opening and vowel neutralization. The modifications begin at correspondingly lower pitches.

### 3.4 Sundberg's Acoustic Analysis of Vowel Modification

Sundberg (multiple publications: 1974, 1977, 1987, 1995) established the acoustic framework for understanding vowel modification:

- Vowel modification is not arbitrary but follows acoustic principles: the singer adjusts vocal tract shape to maintain resonance support for the voice source.
- In classical singing, F1 tuning is the primary strategy in the upper range. Below the passaggio, formant tuning may focus on aligning formants with higher harmonics (R1:2f0 or R1:3f0 tuning).
- Sundberg (1987) demonstrated that the perceived "covering" of the voice in the classical male passaggio corresponds to a combination of vowel modification (raising F1) and laryngeal lowering (which extends the vocal tract, lowering all formant frequencies slightly). The net effect is to maintain R1:f0 or R1:2f0 coupling while producing a "darker" vowel quality.

### 3.5 Vowel Modification in Classical vs. CCM

The acoustic goals and methods of vowel modification differ significantly between classical and CCM:

| Parameter | Classical | CCM / Belt |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Primary strategy | R1:f0 (sopranos high range), R1:nf0 (lower voices) | R1:2f0 (belt); or minimal modification (mix) |
| Laryngeal position | Lowered (extends vocal tract) | Neutral to raised (shortens vocal tract) |
| Jaw opening | Progressive with pitch | Wide for belt, variable for mix |
| Vowel quality goal | Modified vowels acceptable; "beautiful tone" prioritized | Speech-like vowels prioritized; text clarity valued |
| Covering | Yes — darkening via laryngeal lowering + vowel modification | Generally not employed; "open" sound preferred |
| F2 management | Relatively free | More constrained — maintaining speech-like F2 preserves vowel identity |

Titze (2008, JASA) noted that CCM/belt singers use a fundamentally different resonance strategy than classical singers. Rather than tuning R1:f0, belt singers tune R1:2f0 — aligning the first formant with the second harmonic. This:
- Amplifies a different component of the spectrum (giving a brighter quality)
- Requires a higher F1 (wider jaw, raised larynx), achieved by the "megaphone" configuration
- Allows maintenance of speech-like vowel quality somewhat higher in the range than classical tuning would

### 3.6 The "Covering" Concept (Operatic Tradition)

"Covering" (Italian: copertura, or voce coperta) is the classical operatic strategy for negotiating the male passaggio:

- Miller (1993, "Training Tenor Voices," Schirmer) described covering as a complex of adjustments: slight laryngeal lowering, increased pharyngeal space, vowel modification toward more neutral (slightly darker) vowel quality, and often a shift toward mixed registration.
- Acoustically, covering corresponds to maintaining formant-harmonic coupling while transitioning toward a more M2-influenced laryngeal mechanism. The darkened vowel quality reflects the lowered formant frequencies from the lengthened vocal tract.
- The alternative to covering — "open" production through the passaggio — maintains speech-like vowels but typically results in a register break, excessive effort, or a "spread" quality as the singer pushes M1 above its comfortable range without resonance support.

### 3.7 Vowel Modification and Intelligibility

Benolken and Swanson (1990, Journal of Voice) and subsequent studies examined the tension between vowel modification and text intelligibility:

- Vowel modification inherently reduces vowel distinctiveness, as all vowels converge toward more open, centralized positions
- Listeners compensate somewhat by using consonant context, linguistic expectations, and musical context to identify words
- Soprano text intelligibility decreases significantly above approximately G5 (784 Hz), where vowel spaces become highly compressed
- Some contemporary composers write specifically for the acoustic properties of high soprano vowels, treating the voice as a timbral instrument rather than a text-delivery vehicle

### 3.8 Quantitative Formant Data

Smith and Wolfe (various, JASA) measured resonance frequencies in real time during singing using acoustic impedance methods:

- Confirmed that trained sopranos maintain R1 within approximately +/- 10% of f0 in the upper range
- Showed that R2 (second resonance) tuning also occurs but is less systematic than R1 tuning
- Found that the precision of resonance tuning correlates with training level — more trained singers show tighter R1:f0 tracking

Garnier et al. (2010, JASA) used a similar impedance-based method to study formant tuning in belt singers and found confirmation of the R1:2f0 strategy, with trained belters showing precise alignment of F1 with the second harmonic.

## 4. VOCAL ONSET TYPES

### 4.1 Three Primary Onset Types

Voice science recognizes three primary types of vocal onset (phonation initiation):

#### 4.1.1 Simultaneous Onset (Balanced Onset)

- Adduction of the vocal folds and initiation of airflow occur simultaneously
- The folds come together and begin vibrating in a single coordinated gesture
- Produces a clean, clear beginning to the tone without preceding noise or click
- Acoustically: the voice begins at or near its target amplitude without a preceding burst or aspiration

**Physiological characteristics (Koike, 1967, Journal of the Acoustical Society of America):**
- Moderate prephonatory glottal closure
- Subglottic pressure builds as the folds adduct, and oscillation begins immediately as threshold pressure is reached
- Minimal collision forces at onset

#### 4.1.2 Aspirate Onset (Breathy Onset)

- Airflow begins before the vocal folds fully adduct
- An audible period of aspiration (turbulent airflow) precedes the onset of phonation
- The voice "fades in" from breath to tone
- Acoustically: noise energy precedes periodic voicing; the /h/ phoneme is an aspirate onset

**Physiological characteristics:**
- Vocal folds are abducted (open) when airflow begins
- Folds gradually adduct during exhalation
- Oscillation begins when subglottic pressure and adduction reach phonation threshold
- Low collision forces but potentially high airflow waste

#### 4.1.3 Glottal Onset (Hard Onset, Glottal Attack, Coup de Glotte)

- The vocal folds are firmly adducted before airflow begins
- Subglottic pressure builds behind the closed glottis
- The folds are "blown apart" by the accumulated pressure, initiating vibration with a transient burst
- Produces an audible "click" or "attack" at the beginning of the tone
- This is the phonation type used in the glottal stop [ʔ] phoneme

**Physiological characteristics:**
- Tight prephonatory closure — folds pressed firmly together
- Subglottic pressure accumulates to a high level before phonation begins
- The initial vibratory cycle involves high collision forces as the pressurized air forces the folds apart and they snap back together
- Acoustically: a brief transient (click/pop) followed by rapid onset of periodic vibration

### 4.2 Acoustic and Physiological Measurements

Orlikoff (1991, JASA; and subsequent work) measured multiple onset types in detail:

- **Prephonatory interval**: the time from the onset of expiratory airflow to the first detectable vocal fold vibration. Shortest in glottal onset (<20 ms), moderate in simultaneous onset (~20-40 ms), longest in aspirate onset (can exceed 100 ms).
- **Onset airflow**: Aspirate onset shows the highest peak airflow at onset, as air flows through an open glottis. Glottal onset shows a burst of airflow as pressure is released. Simultaneous onset shows smooth onset of airflow.
- **Initial contact force**: Highest in glottal onset, lowest in aspirate onset.

Verdolini (1998, Journal of Voice; and subsequent work as Verdolini Abbott) used electroglottography (EGG) to characterize the prephonatory adjustment period:

- Simultaneous onset shows a brief, coordinated prephonatory period
- Glottal onset shows a longer prephonatory hold with increasing subglottic pressure
- Aspirate onset shows gradual glottal adduction measurable on EGG before phonation threshold

### 4.3 Efficiency and Injury Risk

#### Simultaneous Onset

- Considered the most efficient onset type by most voice science and pedagogy sources (pedagogy consensus)
- Lowest ratio of expended effort to acoustic output
- Lowest collision forces compared to glottal onset
- Minimal air waste compared to aspirate onset
- Verdolini Abbott and colleagues (2012, Journal of Voice) found that simultaneous onset is associated with lowest phonation threshold pressure — the larynx operates at its most efficient point
- This is the onset type targeted in resonant voice therapy (Verdolini Abbott) and in most voice therapy protocols

#### Aspirate Onset

- Low collision forces — potentially protective for injured or recovering voices
- Used therapeutically in voice rehabilitation as a "gentle start" (Stemple et al., Vocal Function Exercises protocol)
- Disadvantages: air waste, reduced vocal power, potential for incomplete glottal closure patterns to become habitual
- Berry et al. (2001, JASA) showed that aspirate onset produces less efficient aerodynamic-to-acoustic energy conversion than simultaneous onset
- In singing, excessive use of aspirate onset produces a "breathy" quality and reduced projection

**Clinical note:** Aspirate onset is prescribed in voice therapy for patients with hyperfunction/hyperfunctional voice disorders, nodules, or recent phonosurgery — it minimizes collision forces during healing. It is typically used as a therapeutic step toward simultaneous onset, not as a permanent production target.

#### Glottal Onset

- Highest collision forces among the three types
- Associated with the highest vocal fold impact stress at onset
- Jiang and Titze (1994, JASA) modeled vocal fold collision forces and found that hard glottal attack produces impact stresses significantly exceeding those of flow phonation onset
- Chronic habitual use of glottal onset is a recognized risk factor for vocal fold nodules (Hillman et al., 1989, Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders; confirmed in multiple subsequent studies)
- However: occasional glottal onset is a normal part of speech (English uses it at word-initial vowels in emphatic speech) and singing (certain articulation styles, dramatic accent)
- The injury risk is associated with **habitual, frequent** use, not occasional artistic use

### 4.4 Onset Types in Singing Contexts

#### Classical Singing

Garcia (1847/1894, Traité complet de l'art du chant) described three onset types and advocated for what he called the "coup de glotte" — which has been variously interpreted as a glottal onset or a clean simultaneous onset. Garcia's original description likely referred to a firm but not aggressive simultaneous onset (Stark, 1999, "Bel Canto: A History of Vocal Pedagogy").

Modern classical pedagogy generally teaches:
- Simultaneous onset as the default
- Aspirate onset for expressive softness or phrase beginnings requiring gentle entry
- Glottal onset sparingly for dramatic emphasis or linguistic clarity (German diction, initial vowels)

#### CCM Singing

CCM styles employ all three onset types as expressive tools:
- Glottal onset is common in pop, rock, and R&B for emphasis and edge
- Aspirate onset for intimate, breathy quality
- Mix of onsets within phrases for expression

The higher prevalence of glottal onset in CCM has raised clinical concern, though epidemiological data on CCM-specific vocal injury rates is limited compared to classical and musical theater data.

### 4.5 Subglottal Pressure at Onset

Titze (1992, JASA) modeled the relationship between onset type and subglottic pressure:

- Phonation threshold pressure (PTP) is lowest for simultaneous onset with optimal adduction
- Glottal onset requires higher pressure to blow apart firmly adducted folds — but this is "burst" pressure, not sustained PTP
- Aspirate onset begins with subglottic pressure below threshold; phonation occurs as adduction increases to meet the available pressure

The efficiency advantage of simultaneous onset is directly related to operating at the phonation threshold pressure minimum — the point where the least driving pressure is needed to initiate and sustain oscillation.

### 4.6 Onset and Vocal Health

Hillman, Holmberg, Perkell, Walsh, and Vaughan (1989, Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders) found that:
- Patients with vocal nodules used significantly more glottal onsets in habitual speech than matched controls
- After voice therapy (training simultaneous onset), nodule patients reduced glottal onset frequency and showed nodule reduction
- The relationship between onset type and vocal health is consistent with the collision-dose hypothesis: glottal onset increases the cumulative collision force on the vocal fold epithelium

Titze (1994, "Principles of Voice Production") formalized the concept of "vocal dose" — the cumulative mechanical loading on the vocal folds as a function of time, frequency, amplitude, and onset type. Glottal onset increases the "impact dose" component of total vocal dose.

## 5. CROSS-CUTTING THEMES

### 5.1 Interaction Between Topics

These four areas interact in practice:

- **Vibrato + Belt**: Belt typically employs vibrato, though often with different characteristics than classical vibrato. Some belt styles use straight tone on sustained notes with vibrato appearing only at phrase endings. The interaction between high-tension M1 production and vibrato generation is not fully characterized in the literature.

- **Vowel modification + Belt**: Belt's R1:2f0 tuning is itself a form of vowel modification — it requires adjusting vowels toward more open, brighter qualities. However, belt and classical vowel modification serve different acoustic goals and produce different timbral results.

- **Onset + Belt**: Belt is commonly initiated with glottal onset for dramatic effect, which may compound the collision stress of an already high-impact technique. Training simultaneous onset in belt may be particularly important for vocal health.

- **Vibrato + Vowel modification**: As vibrato modulates f0, the optimal formant tuning also fluctuates. The vocal tract partially tracks these f0 oscillations (Sundberg, 1987). In the soprano upper range where R1:f0 tuning is critical, the vibrato-related f0 fluctuations require either very fast formant tracking or acceptance of brief moments of detuning during each vibrato cycle.

### 5.2 Key Researchers and Their Primary Contributions to These Topics

- **Johan Sundberg** (KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm): Acoustic analysis of vibrato parameters and norms; source-filter interaction in singing; soprano vowel modification; singer's formant; extensive quantitative acoustics of the singing voice. Primary publications in JASA and Speech Communication.

- **Ingo Titze** (University of Iowa, NCVS): Biomechanical modeling of vocal fold vibration; vibrato mechanism (CT-TA oscillation model); belt acoustics and formant tuning; vocal dose and injury risk; phonation threshold pressure; the "megaphone" model of belt. Primary publications in JASA, Journal of Voice, and NCVS monographs.

- **Jeannette LoVetri** (Voice Workshop, New York): Functional voice training framework; CCM pedagogy; belt technique as legitimate vocal production requiring specific training; CCM survey documenting pedagogical gaps. Primary publications in Journal of Voice and Journal of Singing.

- **Richard Miller** (Oberlin Conservatory): Vowel modification charts for classical male voices; covering/copertura; register-specific vowel management; international comparison of voice pedagogy traditions. Primary publications in his books ("The Structure of Singing," "Training Tenor Voices," "National Schools of Singing").

- **Katherine Verdolini Abbott** (University of Pittsburgh): Resonant voice therapy; vocal onset optimization; phonation threshold pressure and efficiency; clinical application of voice science to rehabilitation. Primary publications in Journal of Voice and Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research.

## METHODOLOGICAL NOTES

### Study Types Represented

- Acoustic analysis of recordings (Sundberg, Prame): Measurements of f0, formants, spectral characteristics. Generally reliable for acoustic description but cannot directly reveal physiological mechanism.
- EMG (Kochis-Jennings, Hirano, Ludlow): Direct measurement of muscle activity. Invasive (intramuscular electrodes), small samples, but provides direct evidence of muscular involvement. Gold standard for mechanism questions.
- Laryngoscopy/stroboscopy (Yanagisawa): Direct visualization of laryngeal behavior. Subject to the limitation that the scope may alter natural production.
- Acoustic impedance measurement (Joliveau, Smith, Wolfe, Garnier): Non-invasive measurement of vocal tract resonances during singing. Provides direct evidence of formant tuning strategies.
- Biomechanical modeling (Titze): Computational models of vocal fold vibration. Useful for testing mechanistic hypotheses but dependent on model assumptions.
- Perceptual studies (Shipp, Sundberg): Listener ratings. Subject to listener variability and cultural bias.
- Epidemiological/survey (Phyland, LoVetri): Population-level data on vocal health and training practices. Subject to confounding variables.

### Limitations of This Synthesis

1. This synthesis is compiled from training knowledge through early 2025. No live literature search was performed. Specific numbers should be verified against original publications.
2. Some study details (exact sample sizes, specific p-values, exact methodology descriptions) may be approximate. When a study is cited, the finding attribution is high-confidence, but precise quantitative details may require verification.
3. The field is evolving rapidly, particularly regarding CCM/belt research. Publications from 2023-2025 may have updated findings on belt safety, vibrato mechanisms, or vowel modification strategies not captured here.
4. Cross-cultural research on these topics is limited — most findings are based on Western classical and musical theater singing traditions.
