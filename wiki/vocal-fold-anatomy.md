The layered structure of the vocal folds and the muscular mechanisms controlling pitch and adduction.

## Overview

Hirano's body-cover theory (1974), validated through 3D canine hemilarynx modeling, provides the structural basis for laryngeal control. The vocal fold is a layered structure with mechanically distinct regions: the body (thyroarytenoid muscle) and the cover (vocal ligament and overlying epithelium). This layered architecture enables the mucosal wave and determines the range of vibratory behaviors available to the voice.

## Details

### Primary Intrinsic Muscles

Two intrinsic laryngeal muscles are the primary controllers of pitch and register:

- **Cricothyroid (CT)** — elongates and thins the folds, raises fundamental frequency. The primary pitch-raising muscle (anatomical).
- **Thyroarytenoid (TA)** — shortens and thickens the folds, contributes to register character and body stiffness (anatomical).

CT and TA interact nonlinearly: TA contraction reduces eigenfrequencies when the fold is already elongated by strong CT activation, but increases eigenfrequencies when folds are at resting or shortened length. Pitch control is a coordinated interplay of both muscles, not a single-muscle affair (Titze, biomechanical modeling).

### Glottal Adduction Balance

The degree of medial compression — how firmly the folds press together — determines the phonation type:

| Condition | Adduction | Characteristics |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Hyperfunctional / hard onset | Excessive | High collision forces, elevated PTP, phonotrauma risk |
| Balanced / flow phonation | Optimal | Lowest PTP with complete glottal closure |
| Hypofunctional / breathy | Insufficient | Incomplete closure, air waste, weak voice |

The balanced zone — lowest tissue impact with complete closure — corresponds to what pedagogy traditions call "resonant voice" or "flow phonation" (pedagogy consensus).
