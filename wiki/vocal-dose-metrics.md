Quantitative measures of cumulative vocal fold vibration exposure developed by Titze, Svec, and Popolo at the National Center for Voice and Speech, analogous to radiation dosimetry.

## Overview

Titze, Svec, and Popolo (2003, JASA) introduced a set of dose metrics to quantify accumulated vocal fold vibration exposure. Four primary metrics capture different aspects of mechanical loading: time dose, cycle dose, distance dose, and energy dissipation dose. These metrics are measured using ambulatory phonation monitors — neck-mounted accelerometers that record F0 and SPL continuously in real-world conditions (Popolo, Svec, and Titze, 2005, JASA).

## Details

### The Four Dose Metrics

**Time dose (Dt):** Total phonation time in seconds — the cumulative duration of vocal fold vibration. Teachers average roughly 25-35% phonation time during a workday, yielding approximately 2-3 hours of actual phonation in an 8-hour day (Titze et al., 2003, JASA). Limitation: does not account for intensity or frequency.

**Cycle dose (Dc):** Total number of vibratory cycles. Dc = sum of (F0 x duration) for each voiced segment. A soprano singing at 880 Hz accumulates cycles approximately 4 times faster than speaking at ~200 Hz. Estimated 1-2 million cycles per day for average voice use; professional singers in heavy rehearsal may reach several million (Titze et al., 2003, JASA).

**Distance dose (Dd):** Total distance traveled by vocal fold tissue, in meters. Approximated as Dd = sum of (4 x amplitude x F0 x duration) for each voiced segment. Captures cumulative mechanical displacement more accurately than time alone. Estimated 500-1400 meters of tissue displacement per workday for teachers (Titze et al., 2003, JASA).

**Energy dissipation dose (De):** Estimates energy absorbed by vocal fold tissue per unit volume. Proportional to F0-squared x amplitude-squared x time, capturing that higher pitches and louder phonation cause disproportionately more tissue stress. Theoretically the most relevant metric for tissue damage, but also the hardest to measure accurately in vivo. Energy dose varies by orders of magnitude between quiet speech and loud singing (Titze, 2006, NCVS).

### Dose Across Professions

Ambulatory monitoring studies have characterized vocal dose profiles across occupations:

- **Teachers:** Among the highest vocal doses of any profession. Mean phonation time ~4.5 hours/day (Remacle et al., 2012, Journal of Voice). Distance dose ~800 meters/workday (Hunter and Titze, 2010, JSLHR). Voice disorder prevalence 11% current, 57.7% lifetime (Roy et al., 2004, JSLHR), compared to 6.2% current and 28.8% lifetime in non-teachers.
- **Singers:** Highest energy dose due to elevated F0 and SPL during performance, but often lower time dose than all-day speakers. During rehearsal/performance days, cycle dose roughly 2-3 times that of average speakers (Svec, Popolo, and Titze, 2003). Opera singers' SPL during singing: 80-110 dB at 30 cm; a 3-hour opera rehearsal can produce distance doses exceeding a teacher's full workday (Echternach et al., 2014, Journal of Voice).
- **Call center workers:** High time dose (6-8 hours speaking per shift), moderate intensity. Voice problems in 18-20% of workers (Jones et al., 2002, Occupational Medicine).

### Dose-Response Relationship

Hunter and Titze (2010, JSLHR) found a statistically significant but moderate correlation (r approximately 0.3-0.4) between cumulative dose and self-reported vocal fatigue in 57 teachers. As of the published literature through 2024:

- No validated safe threshold values exist for any dose metric.
- Individual variation in tissue resilience is large.
- The relationship between dose and pathology is correlational, not causally established at specific thresholds.
- Titze (2006, NCVS) proposed that vocal dose standards could follow the model of noise exposure limits, but this has not been formally adopted.

### Individual Variation

Titze (1999, NCVS) proposed a "vocal fold resilience" concept: some individuals tolerate high doses without tissue changes while others develop pathology at modest doses. Proposed variables include tissue composition (hyaluronic acid concentration, collagen cross-linking), vascular supply, hydration status, hormonal status, and genetic factors. Verdolini et al. (2006) suggested that lamina propria hyaluronic acid content may be the primary determinant of tissue resilience.

## Open Questions

- Whether specific dose thresholds can predict injury risk for clinical or occupational use remains unestablished.
- The relative predictive value of the four dose metrics for different types of pathology is unknown.
- How training and vocal technique modify the dose-injury relationship (efficient phonation may tolerate higher doses) is an active area of investigation.
