The vocal tract as the resonator/filter subsystem, shaping timbre through formant tuning strategies that vary by voice type and genre.

## Overview

The vocal tract shapes the glottal source into identifiable timbre and vowel quality through formant tuning — the strategic alignment of vocal tract resonance frequencies (formants) with specific harmonics. Sundberg's 1974 JASA paper documented the singer's formant: a cluster of the third through fifth formants near 3 kHz, enabling classical singers to project over orchestral accompaniment without amplification.

## Details

### Resonance Strategies by Voice Type and Genre

| Strategy | Voice Type / Context | Mechanism | Acoustic Effect |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| R1:f₀ tuning | Soprano upper range | Jaw opens, raising first formant to track rising pitch | Maintains resonance support as F₀ exceeds speech F1 |
| R1:nf₀ tuning | Tenors, baritones, basses | F1 aligned with 2nd–3rd harmonic | Multiple harmonics reinforced |
| Singer's formant | Classical lower voices | Epilarynx narrowing + pharyngeal widening | 3 kHz cluster; acoustic projection |
| Belt R1:2f₀ | CCM belt, musical theater | Raised larynx, wide mouth (megaphone shape) | Dominant 2nd harmonic; bright/brassy quality |
| Twang / Ring | CCM, applied contexts | Epilaryngeal/aryepiglottic narrowing | 2–4 kHz energy boost; "cutting" quality |

(Sundberg, 1974, JASA; Titze, 2008, JASA)

### Twang: The Contemporary Equivalent of the Singer's Formant

In CCM, twang achieves equivalent acoustic amplification through a megaphone-shaped vocal tract: epilaryngeal tube narrowing + pharyngeal constriction + wider mouth opening. CT imaging studies confirm twang-like voices show a peak of sound energy around 3 kHz, increased vocal fold adduction, and low-frequency spectral changes. Titze explicitly identifies pharyngeal narrowing as the distinguishing feature of twang from other phonation types. Both the singer's formant (classical) and twang (CCM) are vocal tract solutions to the same acoustic problem: maximizing radiated power for a given aerodynamic input.

### The Singer's Formant

The singer's formant is produced when the ratio of epilarynx tube cross-sectional area to pharynx cross-sectional area is approximately 1:6 or smaller. This creates a resonance peak near 3 kHz — a frequency range where orchestral energy is relatively low, allowing the voice to be perceived above the ensemble (Sundberg, 1974, JASA).

### Dual Function of Resonance Configuration

In Titze's nonlinear source-filter model (2008, JASA), vocal tract configurations that produce acoustic amplification simultaneously reduce phonation threshold pressure by increasing inertive reactance. The optimal vocal tract configuration serves two functions at once:

1. Acoustic amplification — increased output without proportional increase in subglottal pressure.
2. Laryngeal efficiency — reduced PTP through aeroacoustic feedback.

### Resonance Adjustments During Dynamic Changes

Traser et al. (2023, JASA) used real-time MRI across 12 professional singers during messa di voce and found close associations between SPL and five vocal tract parameters: lip opening, jaw opening, pharynx width, uvula elevation, and vertical larynx position. All five dimensions co-vary with volume changes — resonance configuration is not static but continuously adjusted during dynamic singing.
