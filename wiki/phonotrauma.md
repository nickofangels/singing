Mechanical injury to vocal fold tissue caused by phonation — encompassing nodules, polyps, hemorrhage, and related lesions, with distinct pathogenetic mechanisms for each.

## Overview

Phonotrauma refers to vocal fold tissue injury resulting from the mechanical forces of phonation. During each vibratory cycle, the vocal folds experience collision force (compressive impact at closure), shear stress (tangential sliding of cover over body during the mucosal wave), and tensile stress. These forces are normal to phonation; pathology occurs when cumulative or acute loading exceeds tissue repair capacity.

## Details

### Mechanical Forces in Phonation

Titze (1994, "Principles of Voice Production") described three primary mechanical stresses on vocal fold tissue:

**Collision force and impact stress:** At vocal fold closure during each cycle, the medial surfaces collide, generating a compressive stress wave. Impact stress (collision force per unit area of contact) ranges from roughly 0.5-5 kPa during normal phonation to potentially exceeding 10 kPa during loud phonation (Jiang and Titze, 1994, JASA). At typical speaking F0 (~120 Hz male, ~200 Hz female), collision occurs 120-200 times per second — exceeding 1 million collisions per full workday (anatomical).

**Shear stress:** During the mucosal wave, the cover layer slides over the body layer. Repeated shear cycling of the lamina propria is a proposed primary mechanism for extracellular matrix breakdown (Titze, 1994).

**Stress distribution:** Gunter et al. (2005, Journal of Biomechanical Engineering) used finite element modeling and found that impact stress concentrates at the midpoint of the membranous vocal fold — the most common site of nodule formation — and at the junction between the anterior one-third and posterior two-thirds.

Contact quotient (the fraction of each cycle in the closed phase) correlates with impact stress: higher CQ = more forceful, prolonged collision = greater tissue loading (Verdolini et al., 1998, 1999, Journal of Voice).

### Vocal Nodules

Nodules are bilateral, symmetric, callous-like lesions at the mid-membranous vocal fold. The dominant theory (Dikkers and Nikkels, 1995, Annals of ORL; Gray and Titze, 1988) is a wound-healing response to chronic repetitive collision trauma (anatomical):

1. Repeated impact stress causes microtrauma to the basement membrane zone.
2. Inflammatory cascade: edema, fibronectin deposition.
3. With continued loading, tissue remodels: basement membrane zone thickens, collagen deposition increases in the superficial lamina propria.
4. Mature nodules show organized fibrous tissue replacing normal loose SLP.
5. Stiffened tissue impairs mucosal wave propagation, producing hoarseness and breathiness.

Behavioral correlates: loud/pressed phonation, hard glottal attacks, excessive duration, high habitual pitch in some cases. Most common in women aged 20-50 (Herrington-Hall et al., 1988, Journal of Voice) — reflecting higher F0 (more cycles) and possible hormonal/tissue factors. In children, most common in boys (behavioral patterns). Early-stage (soft/edematous) nodules are typically reversible with behavioral voice therapy; mature fibrotic nodules may require surgery (Zeitels and Hillman, 2002, Annals of ORL).

### Vocal Fold Polyps

Polyps are typically unilateral, sessile or pedunculated, with a different pathogenesis from nodules:

- **Hemorrhagic origin theory:** Courey et al. (1996, Annals of ORL) proposed that many polyps originate from a submucosal hemorrhage — rupture of a blood vessel in the superficial lamina propria. If the hemorrhage does not fully resorb, the organizing hematoma develops into a polyp.
- Polyps may result from a single acute phonotraumatic event (screaming, forceful coughing) rather than chronic abuse.
- Polyps contain dilated blood vessels, fibrin, edematous stroma, and variable inflammatory infiltrate.
- Behavioral associations: acute phonotraumatic events, phonation during URI (inflamed, vasodilated tissue), smoking, coughing, and throat clearing.

### Vocal Fold Hemorrhage

Hemorrhage involves rupture of submucosal blood vessels in the superficial lamina propria during forceful phonation.

Bastian et al. (1990, Journal of Voice) described hemorrhage in singers as often associated with a sudden voice change during singing ("crack" or sudden quality loss). Risk factors include (multiple sources):

- Upper respiratory infection (edematous, friable tissue with dilated capillaries)
- Menstruation — Abitbol et al. (1999, Journal of Voice) documented that premenstrual hormonal changes lead to vocal fold edema and vascular engorgement, increasing hemorrhage risk
- Aspirin/NSAID use (antiplatelet effects)
- Anticoagulant medications
- Dehydration (increased tissue fragility)
- Vocal fold varices (ectatic vessels predisposing to rupture)

Phonating through acute hemorrhage risks extension of the hemorrhage, organization into a polyp, and permanent scarring of the superficial lamina propria (Zeitels et al., 1999, Annals of ORL).

### Reinke's Edema

Chronic accumulation of fluid in the superficial lamina propria (Reinke's space), causing diffuse polypoid swelling. Strongly associated with smoking (Sato et al., 1999, Annals of ORL found altered extracellular matrix composition). Also associated with chronic vocal abuse, hypothyroidism, and laryngopharyngeal reflux. Results in very low habitual pitch, rough quality, reduced pitch range.

## Open Questions

- Precise threshold at which cumulative dose transitions from adaptive loading to pathological overload is not established for any lesion type.
- Why some individuals develop nodules while others develop polyps or hemorrhage under similar loading conditions is poorly understood.
- The role of genetic variation in extracellular matrix composition as a determinant of injury susceptibility remains largely uncharacterized.
