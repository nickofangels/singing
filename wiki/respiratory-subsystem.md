The respiratory system as the power source of vocal production, generating and regulating subglottal pressure.

## Overview

In the three-subsystem model of voice production (power–vibrator–resonator), the respiratory system is the power source. Its role in singing and speech is not to supply large volumes of air, but to control subglottal pressure — the pressure below the glottis that drives vocal fold oscillation — with precision across a breath phrase.

## Details

### Subglottic Pressure Physics

Subglottic pressure (Psub) is the primary driver of vocal fold oscillation. Glottal source power increases by approximately 6 dB for every doubling of lung pressure above phonation threshold pressure (NCVS). During phonation, Psub ranges from approximately 5–10 cmH₂O for piano to 20–40 cmH₂O for fortissimo singing (Titze, 1989). Titze (1989) also quantified that a rise of approximately 2–6 Hz per cmH₂O of Psub occurs in fundamental frequency during chest register phonation, due to amplitude-frequency coupling in the vocal fold tissue itself.

Sundberg stated this directly: "An increase in subglottic pressure leads to an increase in the airflow — but only if glottal resistance is kept constant." Vocal power demands regulation, not raw exhalation force (pedagogy consensus).

### Subglottal Pressure Management

"Breath support" in pedagogical terminology corresponds to the regulation of subglottal pressure through coordinated action of three muscle groups operating in antagonistic balance:

**Diaphragmatic braking** — The diaphragm actively opposes expiratory recoil during phonation, rather than passively releasing. Watson and Hixon (1985, JSHR) established that trained singers initiate phrases at 70–100% of vital capacity, where passive expiratory recoil can reach ~30 cmH₂O — far exceeding singing pressure requirements. The diaphragm contracts approximately 7.6 cm deeper during active singing inspiration than during passive breathing (Watson & Hixon, 1985). Traser et al. (2020, Radiology) confirmed via dynamic MRI that the posterior diaphragm moves 2× more than the anterior diaphragm and 4–5× more than the rib cage during pitch jumps — indicating fine-grained, differential control.

**Intercostal antagonism** — Internal and external intercostals operate in slight mutual opposition, delaying the rib cage's return to pre-inspiratory position. Salomoni et al. (2016, Journal of Voice) found that classical singers demonstrate asynchronous rib cage-abdomen coordination and significantly lower mean airflow during singing than untrained individuals.

**Abdominal drive** — EMG studies (University of North Dakota, 2003) confirm abdominal muscle activation scales with vocal intensity across pianissimo, mezzo forte, and fortissimo on the same pitch. Macdonald and Blake (2012, Journal of Voice) found consistent recruitment of the transversus abdominis (TAM) and internal oblique (IOM) in 25 professional vocalists via ultrasound. Lateral abdominal activity (obliques), not rectus abdominis bracing, characterizes efficient classical singing (multiple EMG studies). Salomoni et al. (2016) documented pre-phonatory inward abdominal wall movement in classical singers — a preparatory deep core activation absent in untrained individuals.

These three mechanisms constitute the physiological basis of what Italian bel canto calls appoggio and the German tradition calls Stütze/Atemstütze.

### Downstream Effects on the Larynx

Properly managed subglottal pressure reduces the need for excessive laryngeal muscle tension to compensate for erratic air supply. This allows the vocal folds to achieve balanced adduction with lower effort. The respiratory-laryngeal system is coordinately controlled, not serially stacked — dynamic MRI studies of pitch jumps confirm that both ribcage and abdominal muscles participate differentially in managing subglottal pressure across register changes and loud phonation.

### Pathological Patterns

- **Excess airflow with poor pressure regulation** correlates with hypofunctional phonation — incomplete glottal closure, breathy quality, air waste.
- **Inadequate subglottal pressure** correlates with compensatory laryngeal hyperfunction — the larynx compensates for insufficient air support by increasing medial compression, elevating collision forces and phonotrauma risk.

### Proprioceptive Reliability

Watson and Hixon (1985) noted that "subjects' descriptions of how they thought they breathed during singing bore little correspondence to how they actually breathed." Internal proprioceptive awareness of respiratory mechanics is unreliable even in trained singers (Watson & Hixon, 1985, JSHR).

## Open Questions

- The relative contribution of diaphragmatic braking vs. intercostal antagonism vs. abdominal drive likely varies by phrase position, dynamic level, and voice type, but quantitative partitioning has not been established in a single comprehensive study.
- Evidence supporting respiratory exercises specifically for healthy professional voices (rather than disordered populations) is limited (2019 Journal of Voice systematic review).
