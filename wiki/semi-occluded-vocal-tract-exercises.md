Phonation through a partial lip obstruction — one of the highest-transfer training interventions in voice science.

## Overview

Semi-occluded vocal tract exercises (SOVTEs) encompass any phonation through a partial obstruction at the lips: straw phonation (especially flow-resistant tubes), lip trills, tongue trills, cup bubble phonation, and humming. They are among the most studied and broadly effective voice training interventions, with cross-disciplinary support from vocology, speech pathology, and vocal pedagogy.

## Details

### Mechanism

Titze (2007, Logopedics Phoniatrics Vocology; 2020, JSLHR) showed that semi-occlusion raises the inertive reactance of the vocal tract across the 200–1000 Hz range. When the epilarynx tube is simultaneously narrowed, back-pressure builds in the supraglottal airway and acts as an acoustic load that facilitates vocal fold vibration at lower subglottal pressure — reducing phonation threshold pressure.

A narrow tube or straw (3 mm internal diameter or less) produces the strongest effects; greater tube length can partially compensate for wider diameter (Titze, 2020, JSLHR).

### Multi-Subsystem Effects

SOVTEs simultaneously:

1. Reduce phonation threshold pressure
2. Reduce vocal fold collision forces
3. Train the sensorimotor system to phonate at lower effort
4. Facilitate access to mixed registration
5. Provide proprioceptive biofeedback through face vibration

### Impedance Matching and Aerodynamic Power Transfer

Titze (2006, JSLHR) demonstrated that SOVT conditions match glottal impedance to vocal tract input impedance, reducing collision force, increasing oscillation amplitude, and improving vocal economy. Titze (2021, JASA) quantified this further: maximum aerodynamic power transfer to the vocal tract requires reduced glottal resistance combined with increased epilaryngeal resistance — the exact condition SOVT exercises create. A 2024 comparative study (Frontiers in Communication) confirmed that flow resistance tube exercises passively expand the airway: 38% increase in pharyngeal area and 73% increase in epilaryngeal space.

### SOVT Variants

In approximate order of occlusion intensity:
- Voiced bilabial trill (lip trill / "motorboat")
- Straw phonation (narrower straw = more impedance; 2.5–3 mm for measurement, 3–4 mm for training)
- Sustained /v/ or /z/
- LaxVox silicone tube in water

### Evidence

A randomized controlled trial (Kapsner-Smith et al., 2015, JSLHR) compared SOVTEs to Vocal Function Exercises in 20 subjects with dysphonia. Both were efficacious, with SOVTEs producing faster subjective improvement. SOVTE warm-up dissipates perceived effort faster post-exercise than oral reading warm-up.

## Open Questions

- Optimal straw diameter, length, and submersion depth for specific voice conditions remain under active investigation.
