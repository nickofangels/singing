Vocal production directed toward a slightly abducted, continuously flowing glottal configuration — a technique for rebalancing hyperfunctional voices.

## Overview

Flow phonation directs vocal production toward a glottal configuration with slightly reduced medial compression and continuous airflow, rebalancing the respiratory-phonatory-resonant interaction toward lower laryngeal resistance. It is the technical instantiation of the MEAD principle that excessive medial compression is inefficient.

## Details

### Mechanism

Lower adduction within the complete-closure window reduces collision forces, tissue trauma, and the contribution of muscle tension to phonation threshold pressure. Flow phonation does not mean breathy phonation — it maintains complete glottal closure while reducing the force of that closure.

### Evidence

A critical review of six studies including three RCTs found suggestive evidence for improvements in voice quality in dysphonic patients (University of Western Ontario, 2018). A more recent RCT confirmed effects on laryngeal physiology and vocal quality in primary muscle tension dysphonia.

### Multi-Subsystem Effects

Flow phonation simultaneously addresses:

1. Respiratory regulation — balances airflow with reduced laryngeal resistance
2. Laryngeal adduction balance — moves from hyperfunctional toward optimal medial compression
3. Vocal tract coordination — lower laryngeal resistance changes the source-tract interaction

It specifically counters the pathological pattern (hyperfunction) that is the most common presentation in voice disorders.

## Open Questions

- Distinguishing flow phonation from breathy phonation in clinical instruction remains a practical challenge — the perceptual boundary is narrow.
