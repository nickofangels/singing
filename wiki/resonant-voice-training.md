Training toward forward-placement, easy phonation with minimum vocal fold impact — the most studied sensory-guided voice training approach.

## Overview

Resonant voice training aims to produce the strongest, cleanest voice with minimum vocal fold impact, using sensory awareness of oral/facial vibrations as feedback. The Lessac-Madsen Resonant Voice Therapy (LMRVT), developed by Katherine Verdolini-Abbott, is the most extensively studied variant.

## Details

### Acoustic Mechanism

Acoustic modeling by Titze (2003, Journal of Voice) showed that resonant voice corresponds to a state of elevated vocal tract inertance, which reduces phonation threshold pressure — the same physical principle as SOVTEs, achieved through a different route. SOVTEs use physical occlusion at the lips; resonant voice achieves elevated inertance through articulatory configuration of the vocal tract.

Sensory referents — forward facial vibrations — serve as biofeedback indicating that the vocal tract has reached the target configuration (Verdolini-Abbott, LMRVT studies).

### Motor Learning Integration

Verdolini-Abbott's double-blinded study demonstrated that sensory processing instructions (attending to tactile/proprioceptive sensation) combined with variable practice produced the strongest learning outcomes for resonant voice. This aligns with Maas et al.'s (2008, AJSLP) motor learning framework, which found external attentional focus and variable practice superior for retention and transfer.

### Multi-Subsystem Effects

Resonant voice training simultaneously targets:

1. Adduction balance — centers between hyper- and hypofunctional extremes
2. Vocal tract configuration — achieves elevated inertance
3. Respiratory coordination — balances airflow with laryngeal resistance
4. Sensorimotor mapping — trains proprioceptive awareness of efficient configurations

It addresses the hyper/hypofunctional continuum from the center, making it applicable across a wide range of starting vocal conditions.
