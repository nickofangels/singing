Three primary vocal onset types — simultaneous, aspirate, and glottal — defined by the timing relationship between vocal fold adduction and airflow initiation, with distinct efficiency and injury-risk profiles.

## Overview

Voice science recognizes three primary types of vocal onset (phonation initiation), differing in the coordination between glottal closure and expiratory airflow. Simultaneous onset, where adduction and airflow coincide, is associated with the lowest phonation threshold pressure and the most efficient aerodynamic-to-acoustic energy conversion. Glottal onset involves the highest collision forces and is a recognized risk factor for vocal fold nodules when used habitually. Aspirate onset involves the lowest collision forces but the least efficient energy transfer.

## Details

### Simultaneous Onset (Balanced Onset)

Adduction of the vocal folds and initiation of airflow occur simultaneously. The folds come together and begin vibrating in a single coordinated gesture, producing a clean beginning to the tone without preceding noise or click.

- Moderate prephonatory glottal closure; subglottic pressure builds as folds adduct, with oscillation beginning immediately at threshold pressure (Koike, 1967, JASA)
- Minimal collision forces at onset
- Prephonatory interval approximately 20-40 ms (Orlikoff, 1991, JASA)
- Associated with the lowest phonation threshold pressure — the larynx operates at its most efficient point (Verdolini Abbott et al., 2012, Journal of Voice)
- The onset type targeted in resonant voice therapy and most voice therapy protocols (pedagogy consensus; clinical consensus)

### Aspirate Onset (Breathy Onset)

Airflow begins before the vocal folds fully adduct. An audible period of aspiration (turbulent airflow) precedes phonation onset. The /h/ phoneme is an aspirate onset.

- Vocal folds are abducted when airflow begins; folds gradually adduct during exhalation; oscillation begins when subglottic pressure and adduction reach phonation threshold
- Low collision forces
- Prephonatory interval can exceed 100 ms (Orlikoff, 1991, JASA)
- Less efficient aerodynamic-to-acoustic energy conversion than simultaneous onset (Berry et al., 2001, JASA)
- High airflow waste; reduced vocal power; potential for incomplete glottal closure patterns to become habitual
- Used therapeutically in voice rehabilitation as a "gentle start" for patients with hyperfunction, nodules, or recent phonosurgery (Stemple et al., Vocal Function Exercises protocol) — it minimizes collision forces during healing and is typically a therapeutic step toward simultaneous onset, not a permanent production target

### Glottal Onset (Hard Onset, Glottal Attack)

The vocal folds are firmly adducted before airflow begins. Subglottic pressure builds behind the closed glottis; the folds are forced apart by accumulated pressure, initiating vibration with a transient burst (audible "click" or "attack"). This is the phonation type used in the glottal stop [ʔ] phoneme.

- Tight prephonatory closure; subglottic pressure accumulates to a high level before phonation
- Prephonatory interval less than 20 ms (Orlikoff, 1991, JASA) — shorter than other onsets due to pre-established closure
- Highest initial contact force among the three types (Jiang and Titze, 1994, JASA)
- Occasional use is a normal part of speech (English word-initial vowels in emphatic speech) and singing (dramatic accent, certain articulation styles)
- Chronic habitual use is a recognized risk factor for vocal fold nodules (Hillman et al., 1989, Journal of Speech and Hearing Disorders; confirmed in multiple subsequent studies)

### Prephonatory Adjustment

Verdolini (1998, Journal of Voice; subsequent work as Verdolini Abbott) used electroglottography (EGG) to characterize the prephonatory period:

- Simultaneous onset shows a brief, coordinated prephonatory period
- Glottal onset shows a longer prephonatory hold with increasing subglottic pressure
- Aspirate onset shows gradual glottal adduction measurable on EGG before phonation threshold is reached

### Onset and Vocal Dose

Titze (1994, "Principles of Voice Production") formalized "vocal dose" — cumulative mechanical loading on the vocal folds as a function of time, frequency, amplitude, and onset type. Glottal onset increases the "impact dose" component of total vocal dose. Hillman et al. (1989) found that patients with vocal nodules used significantly more glottal onsets in habitual speech than matched controls, and that after voice therapy targeting simultaneous onset, nodule patients reduced glottal onset frequency and showed nodule reduction.

### Subglottic Pressure at Onset

Titze (1992, JASA) modeled the relationship between onset type and subglottic pressure. Phonation threshold pressure is lowest for simultaneous onset with optimal adduction. Glottal onset requires higher "burst" pressure to force apart firmly adducted folds (though this is transient, not sustained PTP). Aspirate onset begins below threshold pressure, with phonation occurring as adduction increases to meet available pressure.

### Onset Types in Singing Contexts

**Classical singing:** Garcia (1847/1894) described three onset types and advocated for what he called the "coup de glotte" — variously interpreted as glottal onset or firm simultaneous onset. Modern interpretation (Stark, 1999, "Bel Canto: A History of Vocal Pedagogy") suggests Garcia likely referred to a firm but not aggressive simultaneous onset. Modern classical pedagogy generally teaches simultaneous onset as default, aspirate onset for expressive softness, and glottal onset sparingly for dramatic emphasis or linguistic clarity.

**CCM singing:** All three onset types are used as expressive tools. Glottal onset is common in pop, rock, and R&B for emphasis and edge. Aspirate onset produces intimate, breathy quality. The higher prevalence of glottal onset in CCM has raised clinical concern, though epidemiological data on CCM-specific vocal injury rates is limited.

**Belt context:** Belt is commonly initiated with glottal onset for dramatic effect. Glottal onset in belt compounds the collision stress of an already high-impact technique (Jiang and Titze, 1994, JASA). No published studies specifically quantify the interaction between onset type and belt collision dose, but the additive nature of collision forces from both sources follows from the vocal dose framework (Titze, 1994).

## Open Questions

- Quantitative data on onset-type distribution in different CCM substyles and its correlation with injury rates is limited.
- Whether the "coup de glotte" as originally intended by Garcia was a true glottal onset or a simultaneous onset with firm adduction remains debated historically.
- The interaction between onset type and vocal tract resonance configuration (i.e., whether formant tuning mitigates collision stress from glottal onset) is not well characterized.
