Retrograde flow of gastric contents reaching the larynx and pharynx, causing vocal fold tissue changes distinct from gastroesophageal reflux disease.

## Overview

Laryngopharyngeal reflux (LPR) involves gastric acid, pepsin, and bile salts reaching the laryngeal mucosa. Koufman (1991, Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery) established LPR as a clinical entity distinct from GERD: LPR patients often do not report heartburn, and the laryngeal epithelium is far more vulnerable to refluxate than the esophageal epithelium. As few as 3 reflux episodes per week to the larynx may be pathological, compared to approximately 50 per week considered normal for the esophagus (Koufman, 1991).

## Details

### Tissue Vulnerability

The laryngeal epithelium — pseudostratified ciliated columnar, transitioning to stratified squamous on the vocal fold surface — lacks the protective mechanisms present in esophageal epithelium (bicarbonate secretion, peristaltic clearance, epithelial resistance). This makes laryngeal tissue disproportionately susceptible to refluxate damage (anatomical).

### Laryngeal Findings

Clinical signs attributed to LPR (Koufman, 1991; Belafsky et al., 2001, 2002):
- Posterior laryngeal edema and erythema
- Interarytenoid edema ("pachydermia")
- Vocal fold edema
- Contact ulcers and granulomas (posterior glottis)
- Increased PTP from tissue edema and stiffness
- Chronic throat clearing — which itself constitutes secondary phonotrauma from repetitive high-velocity vocal fold collision

### Pepsin Deposition

Johnston et al. (2004, Annals of ORL) found pepsin deposited in laryngeal tissue even when pH was not acidic. Pepsin can be reactivated by subsequent acid exposure, suggesting that even "non-acid" reflux is pathological to laryngeal tissue.

### Diagnostic Challenges

Debate exists about overdiagnosis. Hicks et al. (2002) found that many signs attributed to LPR (posterior edema, redness) are also present in asymptomatic controls. The Reflux Finding Score (RFS) and Reflux Symptom Index (RSI), developed by Belafsky et al. (2001, 2002, Journal of Voice), are clinical screening tools but have debated specificity.

Koufman et al. (2000) found posterior laryngitis signs in up to 50% of patients presenting with voice complaints — but the clinical significance of findings in the absence of symptoms is contested.

### Relevance to Singers

Sataloff et al. (1997, Journal of Voice) reported LPR in a significant proportion of singers with voice complaints. Proposed contributing factors specific to singers:
- Increased abdominal pressure during supported phonation, particularly breath support techniques involving abdominal compression
- Performance anxiety and pre-performance eating habits
- Irregular meal timing around performance schedules

## Open Questions

- The specificity of clinical LPR signs (posterior edema, erythema) is debated — these findings occur in asymptomatic individuals.
- Whether empirical proton pump inhibitor therapy for suspected LPR produces voice improvement at rates above placebo is an active area of investigation.
- The contribution of pepsin vs. acid vs. bile salts to laryngeal tissue damage is not fully disaggregated.
