The distinct vibratory patterns of the vocal folds, classified by tissue layer dominance.

## Overview

Roubeau, Henrich, and Castellengo (2008, Journal of Voice) established the M0–M3 framework via electroglottographic study of trained and untrained singers. Register identity maps to which tissue layer dominates vibration, not to any single acoustic or perceptual criterion.

## Details

### The M0–M3 Framework

| Mechanism | Common Name | Dominant Tissue Layer | Typical F₀ Range |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| M0 | Vocal fry | Mucosa (extreme damping) | <75 Hz |
| M1 | Chest / Modal | Thyroarytenoid muscle | ~75–350 Hz (men), ~130–550 Hz (women) |
| M2 | Falsetto / Head | Vocal ligament | ~175–600 Hz (men), ~300–1000 Hz (women) |
| M3 | Whistle | Epithelium | >1000 Hz |

M1 and M2 overlap in frequency — the same pitch can be produced in either mechanism (Roubeau et al., 2008, Journal of Voice).

### Register Transitions as Bifurcations

Titze and Svec et al. (1999, JASA) demonstrated that register transitions behave as bifurcations in a dynamical system: a small smooth change in vocal fold tension produces an abrupt register jump. These transitions exhibit hysteresis — the upward transition (M1→M2) occurs at higher tension than the downward transition (M2→M1).

### Mixed Registration

"Mixed" or "blended" registration corresponds to a nearly rectangular, intermediate glottal geometry that reduces phonation threshold pressure at transition pitches (Titze, 2014, JASA). This intermediate state is unstable but can be stabilized through training.

The ability to access and stabilize mixed registration is considered one of the most productive skills in voice development — it expands usable pitch range, removes the high-effort "break," and teaches adduction control across the full frequency range (pedagogy consensus).

## Open Questions

- The M3 (whistle register) mechanism is less well characterized than M0–M2. Multiple competing hypotheses exist for the tissue dynamics involved.
