The minimum subglottal pressure required to initiate and sustain vocal fold oscillation — the single best quantitative measure of vocal efficiency.

## Overview

Phonation threshold pressure (PTP) was formalized by Titze (1988, 1992, JASA) as the minimum subglottal pressure needed to initiate self-sustained vocal fold oscillation. In healthy adults at comfortable pitch, PTP is typically 2–5 cm H₂O. PTP captures the efficiency of the entire voice production system in a single measurable quantity.

## Details

### The PTP Equation

Pth = kt · B · c · (ξ₀₁ + ξ₀₂) / (Lg · T²)

Where:
- **B** = tissue damping
- **c** = mucosal wave velocity
- **ξ₀** = prephonatory glottal half-width
- **Lg** = glottal length
- **T** = fold thickness
- **kt** = constant

### What Raises PTP (Less Efficient)

- Dehydration — increases tissue damping (B) and mucosal wave velocity (c). Excised tissue studies show 4- to 7-fold stiffness increases with dehydration.
- Tissue pathology — scarring, edema, or nodules alter cover mechanics.
- Excessive adduction — increases the closing force that subglottal pressure must overcome.
- High pitch without adequate fold tension — increases ξ₀.

### What Lowers PTP (More Efficient)

- Optimal hydration — maintains tissue pliability.
- Balanced adduction — complete closure without excessive medial compression.
- Elevated vocal tract inertive reactance — via epilaryngeal narrowing, semi-occluded exercises, or resonant voice configuration (Titze, 2008, JASA).
- Warm, pliable tissue — after appropriate warm-up.

### Clinical and Training Significance

Trained singers maintain stable PTP after extended loud vocalization; untrained voices show significant PTP elevation under identical vocal load. This difference reflects trained singers' superior respiratory-laryngeal coordination, tissue conditioning, and resonance use.

PTP is used clinically to track voice therapy outcomes and to quantify vocal efficiency changes over time.

### Warm-Up Effects on PTP

Vocal warm-up itself affects PTP. Elliot, Sundberg, and Gramming (1993) showed that warm-up exercises reduce PTP in voice-disordered patients. Motel et al. (2003) found that warm-up increased PTP at high pitch in sopranos — interpreted as stabilization of vocal fold viscosity rather than tissue loosening. Both findings reflect the importance of managing the threshold through tissue preparation.
