Training smooth transitions between chest (M1) and head/falsetto (M2) register — arguably the highest-leverage single skill for singers.

## Overview

Register integration refers to training smooth, controlled transitions between M1 (chest/modal) and M2 (falsetto/head) register. Classical pedagogy calls this navigating the passaggio; contemporary pedagogy calls it developing "mix voice." The scientific basis is Titze's bi-stable vocal fold adduction model.

## Details

### Scientific Basis

Titze's 2014 JASA paper on bi-stable vocal fold adduction showed computationally and empirically that the intermediate "mixed" state corresponds to a nearly parallel glottal shape with minimal PTP and moderate vocal fold collision. The transition region is inherently unstable: hysteresis means the upward jump (M1→M2) occurs at higher tension than the downward jump (M2→M1) (Titze & Svec et al., 1999, JASA).

This instability can be stabilized through practice that trains the exact adductory geometry and subglottal-pressure coordination needed to traverse the transition smoothly.

### Multi-Subsystem Effects

Developing smooth register transitions simultaneously:

1. Expands usable pitch range
2. Removes the high-effort "break" from the voice
3. Teaches adduction control across the full pitch range
4. Trains the respiratory system to manage changing laryngeal configurations

Smooth passage through both registers means accessing the full frequency-adduction space efficiently, making register integration one of the highest-leverage skills across vocal pedagogy traditions (pedagogy consensus).

### Pedagogical Approaches

Multiple schools address register integration through different exercises, but the underlying goal converges: stabilizing the intermediate glottal geometry at transition pitches. Messa di voce (crescendo-decrescendo on a single pitch), descending scales from M2 into M1, and SOVTE-based glides through the passaggio are commonly used approaches.

## Open Questions

- Whether "mix" represents a true third vibratory mechanism or a trained stabilization of the M1-M2 transition boundary remains debated, though Titze's bi-stable model favors the latter interpretation.
