Seven principles with agreement across speech pathology, vocal pedagogy, laryngology, and acoustic phonetics.

## Overview

These principles are not owned by any single pedagogical school. They appear under different terminology across classical pedagogy, contemporary commercial music (CCM) training, speech-language pathology, and vocology. Each has support from multiple independent research traditions.

## Details

### 1. Efficiency Over Force

The goal of vocal production is the lowest phonation threshold pressure consistent with the target sound — optimizing tissue mechanics, not maximizing effort. This principle appears as "resonant voice," "flow phonation," appoggio, and "vocal economy" in different vocabularies (pedagogy consensus).

### 2. Respiratory Regulation, Not Volume

Breath support is subglottal pressure management, not lung air supply. Excess air with poor pressure regulation correlates with hypofunctional phonation; inadequate pressure correlates with compensatory laryngeal hyperfunction (pedagogy consensus).

### 3. Tissue Health Is Trainable

Hydration, dose management, and appropriate exercise maintain mucosal wave pliability and low PTP. Overdosing degrades tissue; underdosing leads to atrophy. Optimal dosing — analogous to resistance training — builds resilience (clinical evidence, multiple studies).

### 4. Resonance Amplifies Without Adding Laryngeal Force

Source-filter and source-tract interaction allow the vocal tract to increase acoustic output substantially without proportional increases in subglottal pressure. Forward-placement, twang, and ring instructions across traditions all access this mechanism (Titze, 2008, JASA; Sundberg, 1974, JASA).

### 5. Mucosal Wave Pliability Is the Primary Tissue Target

All warm-up, cool-down, hydration, and rest practices serve the goal of maintaining mucosal wave function. Degradation of mucosal wave pliability is the final common pathway for most occupational voice disorders (clinical consensus).

### 6. Register Bridging Is Structurally Trainable

The passaggio/break is a dynamical bifurcation, not an immutable anatomical barrier. Titze's bi-stable adduction model (2014, JASA) explains both why register transitions are abrupt and why they can be smoothed through training.

### 7. Motor Learning Principles Govern Retention

Variability, external focus, reduced feedback frequency, and deliberate practice structure are principles derived from neuroscience with growing evidence in voice (Maas et al., 2008, AJSLP; Verdolini-Abbott LMRVT studies).

### 8. Coordination Over Isolation

Vocal power is the coordinated output of multiple subsystems (respiratory, laryngeal, resonatory, source-filter), not the maximization of any single one. An undertrained voice typically relies on subglottic pressure alone; a trained voice exploits all levels simultaneously (NCVS, Titze, Sundberg — independently described).
