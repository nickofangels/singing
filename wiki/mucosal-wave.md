The traveling surface wave on the vocal fold that enables self-sustained phonation.

## Overview

The mucosal wave is a traveling wave propagating along the medial face of each vocal fold in the inferior-to-superior direction during each vibratory cycle. It arises from the layered mechanical structure of the fold: the stiff body (thyroarytenoid muscle) and the pliable cover (vocal ligament and epithelium) have different mechanical properties, and the discontinuity allows a wave to propagate through the cover.

## Details

The mucosal wave creates the phase difference between the lower and upper fold edges that underlies the convergent-divergent glottal geometry described by Titze (1980). This geometry is the energy-transfer mechanism that sustains phonation under the myoelastic-aerodynamic theory.

### Clinical Significance

Loss or restriction of the mucosal wave — through scarring, Reinke's edema, or suboptimal tissue properties — directly impairs phonation:

- Reduced wave amplitude correlates with rough, strained, effortful voice quality and increased acoustic noise.
- Videostroboscopy evaluates mucosal wave characteristics as the primary indicator of vocal fold tissue health.

### Tissue Pliability

Mucosal wave behavior depends on the viscoelastic properties of the vocal fold cover layer. Factors that affect pliability include:

- **Hydration** — dehydration increases tissue stiffness 4- to 7-fold in excised tissue studies, directly impairing wave propagation (anatomical).
- **Tissue scarring** — fibrosis replaces the layered structure with undifferentiated collagen, restricting or eliminating the wave.
- **Vocal dosing** — extreme overuse degrades tissue pliability; chronic underuse leads to atrophy.

Maintaining mucosal wave pliability is considered the primary tissue-health goal across vocal pedagogy, speech pathology, and laryngology (pedagogy consensus).
