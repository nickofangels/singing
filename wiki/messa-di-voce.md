The crescendo-decrescendo on a sustained pitch — the canonical coordination exercise that simultaneously engages all vocal subsystems.

## Overview

Messa di voce (Italian, "placing the voice") is the exercise of sustaining a single pitch while gradually increasing volume from pianissimo to fortissimo, then decreasing back to pianissimo. It requires simultaneous regulation of subglottic pressure, glottal adduction, and vocal tract resonance, making it the most comprehensive single test and training tool for vocal coordination.

## Details

### Why It Is the Master Exercise

Messa di voce requires all four vocal power mechanisms to operate simultaneously:

1. **Subglottic pressure regulation** — Psub increases from ~5 cmH₂O (piano) to ~20–40 cmH₂O (fortissimo) and back, demanding precise respiratory antagonism throughout.
2. **Continuous glottal adduction adjustment** — medial compression increases with loudness; the singer maintains complete closure without pressing.
3. **Vocal tract re-shaping** — resonance configuration changes with SPL to maintain timbre and pitch stability.
4. **Source-filter coordination** — the vocal tract must adjust to maintain efficient acoustic coupling as the source characteristics change.

Failure at any single subsystem appears immediately as pitch drift, timbre break, or volume discontinuity.

### Research Evidence

Köberlein et al. (2025, PLoS ONE) found that slow messa di voce (3-second crescendo/decrescendo phases) showed instabilities at SPL endpoints in professional singers, while fast messa di voce (1-second phases) was more stable — task speed modulates the difficulty of pressure regulation.

Traser et al. (2023, JASA) used real-time MRI across 12 professional singers during messa di voce and found close associations between SPL and five vocal tract parameters: lip opening, jaw opening, pharynx width, uvula elevation, and vertical larynx position. All five dimensions co-varied with volume changes, confirming that messa di voce is not a purely respiratory exercise — it demands active tract adjustment throughout.

### Diagnostic Value

The messa di voce reveals subsystem weaknesses that isolated exercises mask:

- **Pitch drift during crescendo** indicates inadequate respiratory-laryngeal coordination — subglottic pressure changes are coupling to frequency.
- **Timbre break at moderate volume** suggests a registration transition or resonance adjustment failure.
- **Inability to achieve pianissimo** indicates excess baseline adduction or inability to manage low Psub.
- **Inability to achieve fortissimo without strain** indicates reliance on laryngeal force rather than resonance amplification.

## Open Questions

- Whether slow or fast messa di voce confers greater training benefit is not established — the Köberlein et al. (2025) finding that slow messa di voce is less stable suggests it may be the more demanding (and therefore more training-productive) variant, but this has not been tested in a training study.
