The universally accepted scientific account of how the vocal folds produce sound.

## Overview

The myoelastic-aerodynamic theory (MEAD) holds that vocal fold vibration is a self-sustaining aeroacoustic oscillation, not a neurally triggered event. Proposed by Janwillem van den Berg (1958, Journal of Speech and Hearing Research), MEAD replaced the neurochronaxic theory (Husson), which posited that each vibratory cycle required a recurrent laryngeal nerve impulse — a claim incompatible with nerve conduction rates at vocal frequencies of 80–500 Hz.

## Details

Three interacting forces sustain vocal fold oscillation under MEAD:

1. **Subglottal air pressure** — blows the adducted folds apart when it exceeds their closing force.
2. **Myoelastic recoil** — the elastic return of displaced fold tissue toward midline.
3. **Aerodynamic forces** — originally described as Bernoulli suction, later refined by Titze (1980, JSHR) to the convergent-divergent glottal geometry mechanism.

The intrinsic laryngeal muscles set mechanical conditions for oscillation — tension, mass, stiffness — but do not fire once per cycle.

### Titze's 1980 Refinement

Titze identified that the dominant energy-transfer mechanism is the convergent-divergent geometry of the glottis across the vibratory cycle (JSHR, 1980):

- During the **opening phase**, the folds diverge from the bottom up, creating a net outward aerodynamic force.
- During the **closing phase**, the geometry reverses to convergent, creating a net inward force.

Aerodynamic pressure pushes folds in the direction they are already moving — the physical condition required for sustained energy transfer from airflow to tissue. Excised larynges with no nerve supply, driven by airflow alone, phonate — confirming that MEAD is quantitatively sufficient, not merely a qualitative narrative.
