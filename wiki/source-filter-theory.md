The acoustic framework describing how the glottal source and vocal tract filter interact to produce voice.

## Overview

Source-filter theory, formalized by Gunnar Fant in *Acoustic Theory of Speech Production* (1960), describes voice production as two separable components: the glottal source produces a harmonic series (energy at fundamental frequency f₀ and all integer multiples), and the vocal tract acts as an acoustic filter whose resonances (formants) selectively amplify harmonics near their center frequencies.

## Details

### Classical Linear Model

In the classical version, source and filter operate independently — vocal tract shape determines vowel quality and timbre, but does not influence how the vocal folds vibrate. This simplification is useful for speech acoustics and vowel analysis but incomplete for singing and clinical applications.

### Nonlinear Source-Filter Interaction

Titze's 2008 paper (Journal of the Acoustical Society of America) demonstrated that source-filter interaction is real and exploitable. The epilarynx tube — the narrow constriction just above the vocal folds — raises the inertive reactance of the supraglottal vocal tract, creating acoustic feedback that drives the folds with greater efficiency.

Narrowing the epilarynx provides impedance matching between the larynx and the broader vocal tract, allowing harmonics to be reinforced without the instabilities that occur when tuned exactly to formant peaks. Both classical singing and contemporary commercial music (CCM) techniques like twang involve epilaryngeal configuration — they exploit source-tract coupling for acoustic output without proportional increases in subglottal pressure or muscular effort (Titze, 2008, JASA).

### The Epilarynx as Trumpet Mouthpiece

Titze (1997, NCVS) described the epilarynx tube's role through analogy: "the inertance of the vocal tract facilitates vocal fold vibration by lowering the oscillation threshold pressure. Not only does the epilarynx tube produce the desirable singer's formant (vocal ring), but it acts like the mouthpiece of a trumpet to shape the flow and influence the mode of vibration." The epilarynx tube, being narrow, creates acoustic impedance comparable to glottal impedance — the condition for strong source-filter interaction.

### Practical Implications

The nonlinear model means that vocal tract configuration simultaneously serves two functions:

1. **Acoustic amplification** — formant tuning shapes timbre and projection.
2. **Laryngeal efficiency** — elevated inertive reactance reduces phonation threshold pressure.

This dual function is the acoustic basis for resonance-based training approaches. Titze (2021, JASA) quantified the efficiency condition: maximum aerodynamic power transfers to the vocal tract when glottal airflow resistance is reduced while epilaryngeal airway resistance is increased.
