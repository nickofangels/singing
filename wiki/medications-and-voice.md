Pharmaceutical agents that affect vocal fold tissue, laryngeal function, or voice production through drying, irritation, hormonal, neuromuscular, or vascular mechanisms.

## Overview

Many common medications have documented effects on voice production. Abaza et al. (2007, Otolaryngology-Head and Neck Surgery) published a comprehensive review categorizing these by mechanism. The most clinically relevant categories for voice users are drying agents (anticholinergics), inhaled corticosteroids, hormonal agents, and medications affecting bleeding risk.

## Details

### Drying Agents (Anticholinergic Effects)

Medications with anticholinergic properties reduce secretions throughout the respiratory tract, including the laryngeal mucosa, increasing tissue viscosity and PTP:

- **Antihistamines:** Diphenhydramine, loratadine, cetirizine — reduce mucosal secretions
- **Decongestants:** Pseudoephedrine — vasoconstrictive and drying
- **Tricyclic antidepressants:** Anticholinergic side effects
- **Some antihypertensives and diuretics**

### Inhaled Corticosteroids

Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) for asthma are associated with dysphonia in 5-50% of users (Williamson et al., 1995, Thorax). Mechanisms include local candidiasis (thrush), mucosal irritation, and possible vocal fold myopathy from steroid deposition on the vocal folds during inhalation.

### ACE Inhibitors

ACE inhibitors cause chronic cough in approximately 10-15% of users. Coughing constitutes repeated high-force phonotrauma — high-velocity vocal fold collision at forces exceeding normal speech.

### Hormonal Agents

- **Androgens** (testosterone, anabolic steroids): Cause irreversible vocal fold thickening and pitch lowering in females (Damrose, 2009, Laryngoscope).
- **Oral contraceptives:** Historical high-androgen formulations occasionally caused voice changes; modern low-dose formulations have minimal documented voice effects (La et al., 2007).

### Bleeding Risk Medications

Aspirin, NSAIDs, and anticoagulants increase the risk of vocal fold hemorrhage by impairing platelet function or clotting cascade. Relevant for all voice users but particularly for those engaged in high-intensity phonation.

### Reflux Medications

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) and H2 blockers treat laryngopharyngeal reflux by reducing acid production, indirectly benefiting voice by reducing laryngeal tissue irritation and edema.

## Open Questions

- Dose-response relationships between specific medications and voice changes are not well characterized for most drug classes.
- Whether newer antihistamines (second and third generation) produce clinically significant vocal fold drying at standard doses is debated.
- Interaction effects between multiple medications with voice-affecting properties are largely unstudied.
